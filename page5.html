As a visual person, the process of making a physical object in real time helped me understand my practice by slowing down my thinking and grounding it in material experimentation. Since we spend so much time on our laptops, it felt refreshing to let my brain play. Showing the object in different settings also revealed how context completely shifts perception, a piece of work feels very different in a gallery than on Instagram. Setting is especially important for my Unit 6 project, a stop-motion film exploring Johari’s Window and how our self image forms through the opinions of others. The work needs time and space for viewers to reflect, rewatch, and piece together the message. I imagine it looping on a projector in a gallery so people can absorb the full picture. This matters because audience engagement changes across platforms; on social media, people often scroll past without experiencing the atmosphere of the work. Working physically will continue to influence my process, reminding me that design isn’t just about what I make, but how and where people encounter it. I want to see my work projected on a big screen to let other designers, general viewers into my world especially with my Time and Movement project 2, it belongs on a big screen for everyone to see. Showcasing me as a designer has been a difficult task as I struggle to see myself as one and or call myself one. I’ve been studying this art for 6 years and calling myself an ‘artist’ still feels alien. Dominique de comarmond, Creative Coordinator of Studio 73 wrote in an article The Importance Of Presenting Your Artwork she discusses the importance of lighting, colours, quality and overall how these factors can vastly change the outlook people have on your work. Message is everything when it comes to our art and so is audience and to get your target audience you need to not necessarily cater to them but keep in mind who you're designing for in the end ‘Some of the things may be that they have already defined their target audience and no matter the quality, their audience is there for it. Others may have been shared through an influential person and get the recognition that way or perhaps the artist owns their brand or "raw' shots. The list can go on and on. So the importance of quality comes into the question of 'How do I want my art to be seen?'. Do you want your piece to stand out and have a more professional shot or something a bit more’ . In the end space and where we present and how we present our work is imperative to the message we want to send and how it will be conveyed by the overseer.
